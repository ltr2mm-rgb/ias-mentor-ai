# Polity & Governance — Revision Notes

*Original study notes for UPSC preparation. Facts are standard and verifiable; the wording is our own.*

## Constitutional Framework

The Constitution of India was adopted by the Constituent Assembly on 26 November 1949 and came into force on 26 January 1950. Dr. B. R. Ambedkar chaired the Drafting Committee and is widely regarded as the chief architect of the Constitution. The Assembly first met in December 1946 and took just under three years to complete its work.

The Constitution originally had 395 Articles in 22 Parts with 8 Schedules. Through amendments it has grown, and today it contains around 470 Articles, 25 Parts, and 12 Schedules — making it one of the longest written constitutions in the world.

Key sources borrowed from other systems: the parliamentary form of government and rule of law from Britain; the idea of a written constitution and judicial review from the United States; the Directive Principles from Ireland; the concurrent list and the idea of a strong centre from the Government of India Act 1935 and Australia; and Fundamental Duties and the ideals of the Preamble drew on multiple traditions.

## The Preamble

The Preamble declares India a Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic Republic and lists the objectives of Justice, Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity. The words "Socialist," "Secular," and "Integrity" were added by the 42nd Amendment in 1976. The Supreme Court held in the *Kesavananda Bharati* case (1973) that the Preamble is part of the Constitution and can be amended, but the "basic structure" cannot be destroyed.

## Fundamental Rights (Part III, Articles 12–35)

Fundamental Rights are justiciable — a citizen can approach the courts directly under Article 32 (called the "heart and soul" of the Constitution by Ambedkar) to enforce them. The six categories are:

- Right to Equality (Articles 14–18)
- Right to Freedom (Articles 19–22)
- Right against Exploitation (Articles 23–24)
- Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25–28)
- Cultural and Educational Rights (Articles 29–30)
- Right to Constitutional Remedies (Article 32)

The Right to Property was removed from the list of Fundamental Rights by the 44th Amendment (1978) and made a legal right under Article 300A. The Right to Education was inserted as Article 21A by the 86th Amendment (2002).

## Directive Principles of State Policy (Part IV, Articles 36–51)

Directive Principles are non-justiciable guidelines to the State for establishing social and economic democracy. They are inspired by the Irish Constitution. Examples include equal pay for equal work, organisation of village panchayats, promotion of cottage industries, protection of the environment (Article 48A), and separation of the judiciary from the executive. Where a conflict arises, the courts have generally sought to harmonise Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles rather than treat one as superior.

## Fundamental Duties (Part IVA, Article 51A)

Added by the 42nd Amendment (1976) on the recommendation of the Swaran Singh Committee, originally ten duties; an eleventh (providing education to one's child) was added by the 86th Amendment. Duties are non-justiciable.

## Union Executive and Parliament

The President is the constitutional head of the Union and is elected indirectly by an electoral college of elected members of Parliament and State legislative assemblies through proportional representation by single transferable vote. The real executive power rests with the Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister, which is collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha (Article 75).

Parliament consists of the President, the Lok Sabha (House of the People, max strength 552), and the Rajya Sabha (Council of States, max 250). The Lok Sabha is directly elected for five years; the Rajya Sabha is a permanent body with one-third of members retiring every two years. A Money Bill (Article 110) can be introduced only in the Lok Sabha and the Rajya Sabha can only make recommendations within 14 days.

## Judiciary

India has an integrated and independent judiciary with the Supreme Court at the apex, High Courts at the state level, and subordinate courts below. The Supreme Court exercises original, appellate, and advisory jurisdiction. Judicial review allows courts to examine the constitutionality of laws. The "basic structure doctrine," evolved in *Kesavananda Bharati* (1973), holds that Parliament can amend the Constitution but cannot alter its essential features such as supremacy of the Constitution, secularism, federalism, and judicial review.

## Federalism and Centre–State Relations

India is described as a "Union of States" (Article 1) with a federal structure that has a strong unitary bias. Powers are divided through three lists in the Seventh Schedule — Union, State, and Concurrent. Residuary powers rest with the Centre. During an emergency the federal structure can become highly centralised. Key institutions of cooperative federalism include the Inter-State Council, the Finance Commission (Article 280), and the GST Council.

## Local Government

The 73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendments (1992) gave constitutional status to Panchayati Raj Institutions (rural) and Municipalities (urban), adding the Eleventh and Twelfth Schedules respectively. They mandate regular elections, reservation for SCs, STs, and women, and State Finance Commissions.

## Emergency Provisions

Three types: National Emergency (Article 352) on grounds of war, external aggression, or armed rebellion; President's Rule / State Emergency (Article 356) on failure of constitutional machinery in a state; and Financial Emergency (Article 360). The 44th Amendment introduced safeguards after the 1975–77 experience, including that the advice for a National Emergency must be in writing by the Cabinet and that "internal disturbance" was replaced by "armed rebellion."

## Constitutional Bodies (quick recall)

Election Commission (Article 324), Comptroller and Auditor General (Article 148), Union and State Public Service Commissions (Articles 315–323), Finance Commission (Article 280), Attorney General (Article 76), and the National Commissions for SCs (338), STs (338A), and Backward Classes (338B). Distinguish these from statutory bodies (e.g., NHRC, CIC) and executive bodies (e.g., NITI Aayog).
