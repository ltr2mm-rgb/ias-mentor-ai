# Economy & Environment — Revision Notes

*Original study notes for UPSC preparation. Facts and definitions are standard and verifiable; the wording is our own.*

## Basic Economic Concepts

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period. GDP can be measured at market prices or at factor cost, and in nominal terms or real terms (adjusted for inflation). Gross National Product (GNP) adds net factor income from abroad. Per capita income is national income divided by population and is a rough indicator of living standards. The economy is conventionally divided into primary (agriculture, mining), secondary (manufacturing), and tertiary (services) sectors, with services contributing the largest share of India's GDP.

## Inflation and Money

Inflation is a sustained rise in the general price level, which erodes purchasing power. It is measured in India mainly through the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the Wholesale Price Index (WPI). Demand-pull inflation arises from excess demand; cost-push inflation from rising input costs. The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) is the monetary authority and uses tools such as the repo rate, reverse repo rate, cash reserve ratio (CRR), and statutory liquidity ratio (SLR) to manage money supply and inflation. India follows a flexible inflation-targeting framework, with the target set in consultation between the government and the RBI.

## Banking and Financial System

The RBI regulates the banking system and acts as the banker to the government, issuer of currency, and lender of last resort. Commercial banks mobilise deposits and provide credit. Priority Sector Lending directs credit to agriculture, small industries, and weaker sections. Financial inclusion initiatives aim to bring the unbanked into the formal system. Non-Performing Assets (NPAs) are loans on which interest or principal is overdue, and high NPAs weaken bank balance sheets.

## Public Finance and Budgeting

The Union Budget is an annual financial statement of the government's receipts and expenditure. Revenue receipts (taxes and non-tax revenue) and capital receipts (borrowings, disinvestment) fund revenue and capital expenditure. Key deficit concepts: the fiscal deficit is the excess of total expenditure over total receipts excluding borrowings; the revenue deficit is the shortfall of revenue receipts against revenue expenditure; the primary deficit is the fiscal deficit minus interest payments. Taxes are direct (income tax, corporate tax) or indirect (GST, customs). The Goods and Services Tax (GST), implemented in 2017, is a comprehensive indirect tax administered through the GST Council, a body of the Centre and states.

## Planning and Growth

India followed Five-Year Plans from 1951 until the Planning Commission was replaced by NITI Aayog in 2015. Landmark reforms came in 1991 with liberalisation, privatisation, and globalisation (the LPG reforms), which opened the economy, dismantled much of the licence-permit system, and integrated India with global markets. Important themes today include employment, the informal sector, agricultural reform, infrastructure, and inclusive growth.

## Environment: Ecology and Ecosystems

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms interacting with their physical environment. Energy flows through food chains and food webs from producers (plants) to consumers to decomposers, with energy decreasing at each trophic level (the "ten percent law"). Biogeochemical cycles (carbon, nitrogen, water) recycle nutrients. Biodiversity refers to the variety of life at genetic, species, and ecosystem levels; India is one of the world's megadiverse countries and has recognised biodiversity hotspots including the Western Ghats, the Eastern Himalayas, the Indo-Burma region, and the Sundaland.

## Conservation Framework

India protects biodiversity through National Parks, Wildlife Sanctuaries, Biosphere Reserves, and community reserves. The Wildlife (Protection) Act 1972, the Forest (Conservation) Act 1980, the Environment (Protection) Act 1986, and the Biological Diversity Act 2002 form the legal backbone. Flagship species programmes include Project Tiger and Project Elephant. Internationally, India is party to conventions such as the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD), CITES (regulating trade in endangered species), the Ramsar Convention (wetlands), and the UNFCCC and its Paris Agreement on climate change.

## Climate Change and Pollution

The greenhouse effect is the trapping of heat by gases such as carbon dioxide, methane, and nitrous oxide; the enhanced greenhouse effect from human activity drives global warming. Consequences include rising sea levels, glacial retreat, and more frequent extreme weather. Pollution — of air, water, and soil — poses major public-health and ecological risks; measures include emission standards, the National Clean Air Programme, and a shift toward renewable energy such as solar and wind. Concepts of sustainable development, the circular economy, and India's commitments to reduce emissions intensity and expand non-fossil energy capacity are frequently tested.
