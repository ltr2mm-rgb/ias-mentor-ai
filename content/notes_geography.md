# Geography — Revision Notes

*Original study notes for UPSC preparation. Facts are standard and verifiable; the wording is our own.*

## The Earth and Its Interior

The Earth's interior is layered into the crust, mantle, and core. The crust and uppermost mantle form the rigid lithosphere, which is broken into tectonic plates that move over the semi-fluid asthenosphere. Plate movements — convergent, divergent, and transform — explain earthquakes, volcanoes, mountain building, and the distribution of continents and oceans. The Himalayas, for instance, are fold mountains formed by the collision of the Indian and Eurasian plates.

## Landforms and Geomorphic Processes

Endogenic forces (originating within the Earth) build landforms through folding, faulting, and volcanism, while exogenic forces (weathering, erosion, and deposition by rivers, glaciers, wind, and waves) wear them down. Rivers create features such as V-shaped valleys, meanders, ox-bow lakes, and deltas; glaciers form U-shaped valleys and moraines; wind action in arid regions produces dunes and mushroom rocks.

## Climatology

The atmosphere is layered into the troposphere (weather occurs here), stratosphere (contains the ozone layer), mesosphere, thermosphere, and exosphere. Insolation, the angle of the Sun's rays, and the distribution of land and water drive temperature patterns. Pressure belts (equatorial low, subtropical high, subpolar low, polar high) and the resulting planetary winds (trade winds, westerlies, polar easterlies) govern global circulation. Cyclones (low pressure) bring stormy weather, while anticyclones (high pressure) bring calm, clear conditions.

## The Indian Monsoon

The monsoon is a seasonal reversal of winds driven by differential heating of land and sea. In summer, intense heating over the Indian subcontinent creates a low-pressure zone that draws in moisture-laden winds from the Indian Ocean, producing the southwest monsoon (June–September), which delivers the bulk of India's annual rainfall. In winter, the pattern reverses, giving the drier northeast monsoon, which brings rain to the southeastern coast (Tamil Nadu). Factors such as the Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone, the Tibetan Plateau, El Niño and La Niña, and the Indian Ocean Dipole influence the strength and timing of the monsoon.

## Indian Physiography

India's major physical divisions are the Himalayan mountains in the north, the Northern Plains formed by the Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra river systems, the Peninsular Plateau (including the Deccan Plateau bounded by the Western and Eastern Ghats), the Thar Desert, the Coastal Plains, and the islands (Andaman & Nicobar in the Bay of Bengal, Lakshadweep in the Arabian Sea). The Western Ghats are older and more continuous than the Eastern Ghats and are a biodiversity hotspot.

## Drainage Systems

Indian rivers are classified as Himalayan (perennial, fed by snowmelt and rain, e.g., Ganga, Yamuna, Brahmaputra) and Peninsular (mostly seasonal, rain-fed, e.g., Godavari, Krishna, Kaveri, Narmada, Tapi). Most peninsular rivers flow eastward into the Bay of Bengal, while the Narmada and Tapi are notable exceptions flowing westward into the Arabian Sea through rift valleys.

## Soils and Agriculture

Major soil types include alluvial soil (most fertile, found in the northern plains and river deltas), black or regur soil (ideal for cotton, found in the Deccan trap region), red soil, laterite soil, arid soil, and mountain soil. Cropping seasons are Kharif (monsoon-sown, e.g., rice, cotton), Rabi (winter-sown, e.g., wheat, mustard), and Zaid (short summer season).

## World Geography and Resources

Key concepts include ocean currents (warm currents like the Gulf Stream and cold currents like the Labrador Current) and their influence on climate and fisheries, the distribution of major biomes (tropical rainforests, savannas, deserts, temperate grasslands, tundra), and the global distribution of minerals and energy resources. Latitudes and longitudes, time zones, and the significance of the equator, tropics, and Prime Meridian are foundational for map-based questions.

## Economic and Human Geography (India)

India's population distribution is shaped by relief, climate, and water availability, with dense settlement in the fertile plains. Important themes include urbanisation, migration, industrial regions (e.g., the Mumbai–Pune and Chota Nagpur belts), transport networks, and the location factors for industries such as iron and steel, cotton textiles, and IT.
