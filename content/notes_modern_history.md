# Modern History — Revision Notes

*Original study notes for UPSC preparation. Facts are standard and verifiable; the wording is our own.*

## The Revolt of 1857

Often called the First War of Independence, the uprising began at Meerut on 10 May 1857 and quickly spread to Delhi, where the rebels proclaimed the aged Mughal emperor Bahadur Shah Zafar as their symbolic leader. Immediate triggers included the greased cartridges of the new Enfield rifle, but deeper causes were economic exploitation, the policy of annexation (the Doctrine of Lapse used by Lord Dalhousie), and social and religious interference. Prominent leaders included Rani Lakshmibai of Jhansi, Nana Sahib and Tantia Tope at Kanpur, and Begum Hazrat Mahal at Lucknow. The revolt was suppressed by 1858, after which the Crown took over direct control from the East India Company through the Government of India Act 1858, and Queen Victoria's Proclamation promised non-interference in religious matters.

## Socio-Religious Reform Movements

The nineteenth century saw reform movements seeking to modernise Indian society. Raja Ram Mohan Roy founded the Brahmo Samaj (1828) and campaigned against Sati, which was abolished in 1829 under Lord William Bentinck. Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar promoted widow remarriage. Swami Dayananda Saraswati founded the Arya Samaj (1875) with the slogan "Back to the Vedas." Swami Vivekananda established the Ramakrishna Mission (1897). Jyotiba Phule worked for the education of women and lower castes and founded the Satyashodhak Samaj. Sir Syed Ahmad Khan led the Aligarh Movement to promote modern education among Muslims.

## Formation of the Indian National Congress

The Indian National Congress was founded in 1885 with A. O. Hume playing a key organising role; its first session was held in Bombay under the presidency of W. C. Bonnerjee. The early phase (1885–1905) was dominated by the "Moderates" — leaders like Dadabhai Naoroji (author of the "drain of wealth" theory), Gopal Krishna Gokhale, and Pherozeshah Mehta — who used constitutional methods, petitions, and prayers.

## The Extremist Phase and Swadeshi

The partition of Bengal in 1905 by Lord Curzon sparked the Swadeshi and Boycott movement. Leaders such as Bal Gangadhar Tilak ("Swaraj is my birthright and I shall have it"), Lala Lajpat Rai, and Bipin Chandra Pal (the "Lal-Bal-Pal" trio) advocated more assertive methods. The Congress split at the Surat session (1907) between Moderates and Extremists. The Muslim League was formed in 1906.

## The Gandhian Era

Mahatma Gandhi returned to India from South Africa in 1915. His early experiments with satyagraha were at Champaran (1917, indigo cultivators), Ahmedabad (1918, mill workers), and Kheda (1918, peasants). The Rowlatt Act (1919) and the Jallianwala Bagh massacre (13 April 1919) intensified nationalist anger.

- **Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–22):** Boycott of schools, courts, titles, and foreign goods; called off after the Chauri Chaura incident in February 1922.
- **Civil Disobedience Movement (1930–34):** Launched with the Dandi Salt March (12 March–6 April 1930) to break the salt law. The Gandhi–Irwin Pact (1931) led to Gandhi's participation in the Second Round Table Conference.
- **Quit India Movement (1942):** Launched on 8 August 1942 with the call "Do or Die"; met with mass repression and the arrest of the entire Congress leadership.

## Constitutional and Political Milestones

Key acts shaping self-government include the Morley–Minto Reforms (1909, separate electorates), the Montagu–Chelmsford Reforms (1919, dyarchy in provinces), the Simon Commission (1928, boycotted as it had no Indian member), the Nehru Report (1928), and the Government of India Act 1935, which introduced provincial autonomy and a proposed federal structure. The demand for a separate nation was articulated in the Lahore Resolution of the Muslim League (1940).

## Towards Independence and Partition

The Cripps Mission (1942) failed to satisfy nationalist demands. After the Second World War, the Cabinet Mission (1946) proposed a framework for a united India that ultimately broke down. The Mountbatten Plan (3 June 1947) accepted partition, and the Indian Independence Act 1947 created the two dominions of India and Pakistan on 15 August 1947. Subhas Chandra Bose's Indian National Army (Azad Hind Fauj) and the naval mutiny of 1946 added pressure in the final years.

## Quick Recall — Important Figures

Dadabhai Naoroji (Grand Old Man of India, drain theory), Gopal Krishna Gokhale (Gandhi's political mentor), Bal Gangadhar Tilak (Lokmanya), Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel (integration of princely states, "Iron Man"), Bhagat Singh and the revolutionary movement, and Maulana Abul Kalam Azad (education and unity).
